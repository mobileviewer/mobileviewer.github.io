How to Use the Contrast Checker

Three simple steps to verify your color combinations meet WCAG accessibility standards.

Step 01

Pick Your Colors

Click the color swatch to open the native color picker, or type any HEX code directly (e.g. #FF5733) into the foreground and background fields. The checker updates in real time as you type.

Step 02

Read the Contrast Ratio

The tool instantly calculates your contrast ratio (e.g. 4.7:1) and shows whether your combination passes or fails WCAG AA and AAA for normal text, large text, and UI components.

Step 03

Adjust & Fix

If your colors fail, scroll down to view accessible color suggestions. Click any suggestion chip to instantly apply it. Use the "Swap" button to test the reverse combination. Copy the final values as CSS variables.

About This Tool

What is the Accessibility Contrast Checker?

The Accessibility Contrast Checker is a free, browser-based tool that calculates the color contrast ratio between any two colors — your text color and its background. This ratio determines whether people with low vision, color blindness, or other visual impairments can clearly read your content.

Built around the WCAG 2.1 (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) standard, the checker tests against all relevant success criteria: AA minimum, AA large text, AAA enhanced, and non-text contrast for UI elements. All calculations run entirely in your browser — no data is sent to any server.

Real-Time Calculation

Contrast ratio updates instantly as you change colors — no page reload or button click needed.

Full WCAG 2.1 Coverage

Tests AA, AA Large, AAA, and AAA Large levels, plus non-text contrast (WCAG 1.4.11) for UI components.

Smart Fix Suggestions

When colors fail, the tool auto-suggests accessible alternatives that preserve your palette's intent while meeting WCAG requirements.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about color contrast, WCAG, and accessibility compliance.

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance requires a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text (below 18pt regular or 14pt bold) and 3:1 for large text (18pt or 14pt bold and above). UI components, icons, and graphical objects also require a 3:1 ratio against adjacent colors under WCAG 1.4.11.
WCAG 2.1 AAA (Enhanced Accessibility) requires a contrast ratio of at least 7:1 for normal text and 4.5:1 for large text. AAA is the highest conformance level and ensures readability for users with severe visual impairments. While not always mandatory, it is strongly recommended for critical content.
The contrast ratio is calculated using the relative luminance formula defined in WCAG 2.1 Success Criterion 1.4.3. First, each RGB channel is converted to a linear value, then combined into a luminance value (L). The ratio is then computed as (L1 + 0.05) / (L2 + 0.05), where L1 is the lighter color's luminance and L2 is the darker color's. The result ranges from 1:1 (no contrast) to 21:1 (black on white).
Yes. Under WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.11 (Non-text Contrast), UI components including form inputs, buttons, focus indicators, and meaningful icons must have a minimum 3:1 contrast ratio against adjacent colors. Purely decorative graphics, logos, and inactive UI elements are exempt from this requirement.
This tool is completely free with no registration required. All contrast calculations happen entirely in your browser using JavaScript — no color data, usage analytics specific to your color choices, or personal information is transmitted to any server. You can also use it offline once the page has loaded.
You can enter colors as HEX codes (e.g. #FF5733 or #F53 shorthand) directly in the input field, or use the native browser color picker which also lets you sample colors from your screen using the eyedropper tool in supported browsers (Chrome, Edge). Future updates will add direct RGB and HSL input support.
Approximately 8% of men and 0.5% of women have some form of color vision deficiency, and millions more experience low vision. Sufficient contrast ensures your text and UI are readable for everyone — regardless of their visual ability, device brightness, or ambient lighting conditions. It also improves SEO, reduces cognitive load, and is a legal requirement under ADA, EN 301 549, and various national accessibility laws.
